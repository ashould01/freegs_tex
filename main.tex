\begin{abstract}
  The Grad--Shafranov equation describes the steady-state ideal magnetohydrodynamic 
  (MHD) equilibrium of a plasma under the assumption of axisymmetry, used to model 
  a steady-state plasma equilibrium in tokamak devices. 
  This study introduces a physics-informed neural operator framework 
  for solving the free-boundary Grad-Shafranov equation, 
  a nonlinear elliptic partial differential equation (PDE)
  whose boundary condition is determined by the solution itself.
  First, we decompose the Grad-Shafranov equation into two sub-problems
  and train two neural operators independently, 
  using physics-informed loss functions without paired solution labels.
  Next, we combine their outputs to obtain the full solution of the 
  one-step Grad-Shafranov equation.
  This model is then integrated into an existing iterative Grad--Shafranov solver,
  to assess whether the solver-in-the-loop surrogate convergences to an accurate equilibrium.
  Validation on the Solov'ev equilibrium shows that the proposed method can provide
  accurate poloidal flux compared to the analytical solution,
  and the validation on MAST-U tokamak equilibrium shows that
  the proposed method can provide accurate poloidal flux for both diverted and limited plasma configurations, and reproduce physically relevant equilibrium quantities.

\section{Introduction}\label{intro}
Accurate knowledge of the plasma equilibrium is essential for 
designing tokamak operating scenarios, and controlling the plasma shape in real time.
From a combination of physical models, machine descriptions, 
and diagnostic measurements, plasma equilibrium reconstruction is done
by estimating the magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) equilibrium state.
In practical tokamak operations, the reconstructed equilibrium provides quantities 
such as the poloidal flux, plasma boundary, magnetic axis, X-points, and current density profile.
These quantities are used not only for offline analysis but also for controlling tasks 
such as plasma shape control and scenario optimization.
Since the equilibrium quantities may need be updated repeatedly during real-time optimization,
such reconstruction must be performed accurately and quickly.

To simplify the problem, the plasma equilibrium is often assumed to be toroidally axisymmetric.
Then the steady-state ideal-MHD equilibrium of a tokamak plasma 
is described by the Grad-Shafranov equation \cite{GRAD,SHAFRANOV}. 
It is nonlinear because the toroidal current density and the plasma boundary 
depend on the poloidal flux function, and as a result, the source term and the boundary condition
must be updated consistently during the solution process.

Classical numerical solvers remain the standard tools for tokamak equilibrium 
reconstruction and simulation, including finite-difference, finite-element, spectral methods.
Also, to improve the reality of the simulation, 
profile parametrizations and diagnostic constraints are needed to ensure physical consistency.
Equilibrium reconstruction codes such as EFIT and related real-time reconstruction methods 
have been widely used in tokamak experiments.
In these solvers, the nonlinear Grad--Shafranov problem is typically solved iteratively.
Given a previous flux estimate, the plasma boundary and current density are updated, 
and a one-step elliptic Grad--Shafranov problem is solved to obtain the next flux iterate.
This repeated one-step solve is a central computational component of the overall equilibrium algorithm.

Conventional Grad--Shafranov solvers face several computational and modeling challenges.
First, the free-boundary problem is nonlinear and may not be a contraction mapping, 
so convergence of a simple fixed-point iteration is not guaranteed in general. 
Second, the boundary calculation couples the interior flux distribution 
with the surrounding tokamak geometry and external currents, 
which complicates the construction of explicit Jacobians. 
Third, real-time reconstruction and large-scale parametric studies require many 
equilibrium solves under varying plasma parameters, coil currents, and boundary configurations.
These considerations motivate the development of fast surrogate models 
that can accelerate repeated equilibrium computation while retaining physical consistency.

Neural networks have recently emerged as promising tools for 
scientific computing. 
In particular, physics-informed neural networks incorporate 
differential-equation residuals and boundary conditions into the training objective, 
allowing neural models to approximate PDE solutions with reduced dependence on labeled data.
Neural operators extend this idea from learning individual solutions 
to learning mappings between function spaces. 
Architectures such as DeepONet and Fourier neural operators have shown that 
solution operators of parametric PDEs can be approximated and evaluated efficiently after training.
Physics-informed neural operators further incorporate governing equations 
into the operator-learning framework, making them attractive for problems 
where generating high-fidelity labeled solution data is expensive.

However, applying neural operators to the free-boundary Grad--Shafranov problem 
is not straightforward. 
A direct neural prediction of the final equilibrium may be deficient, 
because it does not guarantee the satisfaction of the Grad--Shafranov equation,
and it may not generalize reliably when the boundary configuration 
or current-density profile changes. 
In this work, we propose FreeGSNO, a physics-informed neural-operator framework 
for solving the free-boundary Grad--Shafranov equation. 
The key idea is not to replace the entire equilibrium solver by a one-shot predictor, 
but to learn the one-step solution operator that appears inside the 
iterative Grad--Shafranov workflow. 
For a given previous iterate, the one-step problem is decomposed into 
a residual problem with a source term and homogeneous boundary condition, 
and a boundary problem with homogeneous differential equation and 
nonhomogeneous boundary condition. 
We approximate the residual component using a modified physics-informed DeepONet 
whose branch network encodes the discretized toroidal current-density source term 
and whose trunk network encodes spatial coordinates. 
Also we approximate the boundary component using a physics-informed 
neural transformer operator that takes boundary coordinates and boundary values 
as key-value information and predicts the corresponding harmonic contribution inside the domain.

The learned neural operator can be inserted into the existing iteration methods 
for the Grad--Shafranov equation. 
In this work, we integrate the proposed neural operator into a Python-based
FreeGS and FreeGSNKE solver, which follows a fixed-point and Jacobian-free Newton--Krylov workflow,
and we check that the proposed method remains compatible with conventional free-boundary
equilibrium algorithms. 
In this sense, FreeGSNO is a solver-in-the-loop neural surrogate 
rather than a purely data-driven equilibrium predictor.

We evaluate FreeGSNO in two settings. The first is a Solov'ev equilibrium problem, 
where an analytic solution is available and the accuracy of the neural operators 
can be directly measured. 
The second is a realistic MAST-U free-boundary equilibrium setting, 
where the proposed model is integrated with a FreeGSNKE-based workflow and 
compared with the reference numerical solver. 
The results demonstrate that the proposed method can approximate the poloidal flux and 
reproduce physically important equilibrium quantities such as the magnetic axis, 
X-point, and plasma boundary. 
These experiments indicate that physics-informed neural operators can provide 
a promising route toward fast and physically consistent Grad--Shafranov equilibrium computation.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{Numerical Algorithms for  the Grad-Shafranov Equation and Plasma Equilibrium}

A large body of work \cite{Lao_1985,BLUM,RT-EFIT,EFIT++,LIUQE,HAN2021,SPECTRAL,adaptiveGradShafranov,jeon,freegsnke} 
has addressed the numerical solution of the Grad-Shafranov equation for various tokamak devices,
using numerical methods such as finite difference, finite element, and spectral methods.
Since the source term and the plasma boundary depend on the flux itself, 
iterative methods are commonly employed.
Lao et al. \cite{Lao_1985} developed an equilibrium reconstruction framework 
for estimating current-profile parameters and plasma shapes in tokamaks.
Their parametrization of the pressure and 
poloidal-current profiles (Lao-type profiles) has been widely adopted in 
Grad--Shafranov equilibrium reconstruction codes.
\cite{RT-EFIT} proposed a real-time equilibrium reconstruction algorithm for the 
Grad-Shafranov equation in DIII-D \cite{DIIID} tokamak, 
with reusing the previous measurement data to accelerate the computation.
Such real-time reconstruction methods are widely used for plasma shape control in tokamaks.
Recently, a Python based, free-boundary Grad-Shafranov solver 
FreeGS \cite{freegs} has been proposed,
and Amorisco et al. \cite{freegsnke} further developed FreeGSNKE from FreeGS,
which utilizes a Jacobian free Newton-Krylov method.
It uses a finite difference discretization on the computational domain, and uses
both fixed-point iteration and Jacobian-free Newton-Krylov method.
It has been validated \cite{validation} against 
widely used numerical solver EFIT++ \cite{EFIT++} and FIESTA \cite{Fiesta} for MAST-U equilibriua.
In this work, we use FreeGS and FreeGSNKE as our computational framework,
with physics-informed neural solver for the Grad-Shafranov equation.

\subsection{Physics-Informed Neural Operators}

Neural operators (NOs) \cite{DeepONet,FNO,BENO,Transolver,LNO} are a class of neural networks 
designed to learn mappings between function spaces, enabling them to approximate 
the solution operator of PDEs with given input functions.
\cite{DeepONet} proposed DeepONet, which is a neural operator architecture 
based on the universal approximation theorem \cite{UATO} for operators. 
While, Fourier neural operator (FNO) \cite{FNO} is a neural operator architecture
with Fourier-space kernel parametrization.
Variants of the neural operator architecture \cite{NOMAD} have been proposed, for example, 
\cite{NOMAD} interprets the DeepONet structure as an encoder-decoder structure, 
and improves performance by modifying the decoder structure so that it can express 
more complex functions.
Recently, due to the success of the Transformer architecture \cite{transformer} 
in many research areas, neural operator architectures based on the transformer have been proposed.
For example, \cite{BENO} proposed a transformer-based neural operator architecture,
to learn solution operators for PDES with complex geometries and inhomogeneous boundary conditions.

Meanwhile, Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) \cite{PINNs} are a class of neural networks 
that incorporate physical laws and constraints into the training process,
allowing them to learn solutions to partial differential equations 
(PDEs) without relying on labeled data.
\cite{PIDeepONet,PIFNO} combined the above approaches to propose 
physics-informed neural operators (PINOs).
Compared with PINNs, PINOs learn a solution operator rather than a single solution,
therefore it can predict a various solution with various 
input functions without retraining the model.
On the other hand, compared with purely data-driven neural operators, PINOs incorporate
PDE residuals and boundary conditions during training, hence the training process 
does not require labeled data, and it can generalize better to unseen scenarios.
In particular, \cite{PINTO} proposed a physics-informed neural transformer operator (PINTO), 
which is a transformer-based neural operator architecture with a physics-informed loss function.
It can learn the solution operator of PDEs with various boundary value functions, and 
it has been outperformed the previous neural operator architectures in various PDEs.

\subsection{Predicting Plasma Equilibrium with Neural Networks}

Successful applications of neural networks to predict the plasma equilibrium 
\cite{bonotto,gsdeepnet,JangPINN,multistagePINN,summaryNOGS,NNGS,NNFB,PINNFNOGS} has been reported.
Real-time plasma equilibrium reconstruction is important 
for controlling the plasma shape in tokamaks,
and neural networks can serve as fast surrogates or auxiliary models
to reconstruct the plasma equilibrium in real-time.
For example, \cite{bonotto} proposed a neural operator approach 
to predict the plasma equilibrium in ITER-like tokamak.
For given input current and magnetic field measurements, they proposed three neural networks,
each of which predicts the plasma flux function, 
determines plasma boundary value and type, and determines the plasma boundary location, 
by supervised learning with the data generated by the numerical solver.
Data-driven training has a limitation that it requires a large amount of data 
to train the model, and it may not generalize well to unseen scenarios.   
\cite{gsdeepnet} proposed a data-free approach and used only magnetic measurements during training 
process. Two subnetworks are used to predict the plasma flux function and some 
physical quantities, i.e., pressure, and these are used to compute a physics-informed loss  
with the Grad-Shafranov equation.
Also, the predicted magnetic field is compared to the magnetic input information to improve the 
accuracy of the model. 
They validated their model with the real data from KSTAR \cite{KSTAR} tokamak.

Meanwhile, several efforts have also been made to accurately 
obtain the plasma equilibrium reconstruction 
with neural networks, to serve as a simulation tool.
For example, \cite{JangPINN} proposed a physics-informed neural network to predict
the plasma equilibrium for fixed boundary problems with varying boundary shapes,
and compared to the analytical solution obtained by assuming the Solov'ev profile
\cite{Solovev}, to show the possibility of neural networks as a solver for the Grad-Shafranov equation.
To more accurately reconstruct the plasma equilibrium, \cite{multistagePINN} proposed a 
multi-stage PINN, and they obtained the plasma flux function with the absolute error
between the analytic solution and the predicted solution below $10^{-7}$ for the Solov'ev profile.
However, these works are limited to the fixed-boundary problem.
We propose a solution label-free physics-informed neural operator framework to 
predict the plasma equilibrium with a free-boundary condition,
and we integrate it with a numerical solver, obtaining accurate plasma poloidal flux
and generalization ability.

\section{Preliminaries}

In this section, we introduce the free-boundary Grad-Shafranov equation and describe the
neural operator architectures used in this work.

\subsection{Grad-Shafranov Equation}
The Grad-Shafranov equation describes the steady-state plasma equilibrium within
the framework of ideal magnetohydrodynamics (MHD), under the assumption that
the plasma is axisymmetric.

Let $(R, \phi, Z)$ be a cylindrical coordinate system with $R > 0$.
We define $\Omega = (0, R_{max})\times (-Z_{max}, Z_{max})$ as a 2D computational domain 
in the coordinates $(R, Z)$. We also define the plasma domain $\Omega_p\subset \Omega$ 
as the region occupied by the plasma.
The corresponding plasma boundary is denoted by $\partial \Omega_p$.
In many cases, determining or controlling the plasma domain is a central issue
in plasma equilibrium problems.
% We will discuss more about the plasma boundary in the Section~\ref{sec:2.1.2} and 
% Figure~\ref{fig:boundary}.

The Grad-Shafranov equation reads
\begin{align}\label{eq:1}
  \Delta^* \psi(R, Z) = -\mu_0 R J_\phi(R, Z; \psi), \quad (R, Z) \in \Omega,
\end{align}
where $\psi = \psi(R, Z)$ is the axisymmetric poloidal flux function,
$\mu_0$ is the vacuum permeability, and $J_\phi$ is the toroidal current density.
The operator $\Delta^*$ is an elliptic operator with cylindrical coordinates, defined by
$\Delta^* = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial R^2} - \frac{1}{R}\frac{\partial}{\partial R} + 
\frac{\partial^2}{\partial Z^2}$.
To close the equation, we have to determine the toroidal current density $J_\phi$ and the boundary condition.

\subsubsection{Modeling of the toroidal current density $J_\phi$}

The toroidal current density $J_\phi$ depends not only on nonlinear functions of $\psi$ and 
on the plasma boundary, but also on external environments, such as currents in the active coils.
To be more precise, $J_\phi$ can be decomposed into two parts, 
\begin{align}
  J_\phi(R, Z; \psi) = J_{\text{p}, \phi}(R, Z; \psi) + J_{\text{m}}(R, Z),
\end{align}
where $J_{\text{p}, \phi}$ is the plasma current density and 
$J_{\te{m}}$ is the current density due to the external coils. 
For simplicity, we treat $J_\te{m}$ as fixed; in general, however,
$J_\te{m}$ is a main control variable for the plasma equilibrium problems.

The plasma current density $J_{\text{p}, \phi}$ is composed of the pressure $p = p(\psi)$ and the 
toroidal magnetic field function $F = F(\psi)$;
\begin{align}\label{eq:3}
  J_{\te{p}, \phi}(R, Z; \psi) = \begin{cases}
  \begin{aligned}
  & R p'(\psi)(R, Z) + \\
  & \quad \frac{1}{R} \left(F(\psi)F'(\psi)\right)(R, Z)
  \end{aligned} &  (R, Z) \in \Omega_p \\
  0 & (R, Z) \in \Omega \setminus \Omega_p
\end{cases}, \end{align}
where $p'(\psi)$ and $F'(\psi)$ are the derivatives of $p$ and $F$ with respect to $\psi$, respectively. 
Thus, the plasma current density is affected by the plasma boundary. 
In the next subsection, we will discuss how to determine the plasma boundary.

Several models have been proposed for $p'(\psi)$ and $F'(\psi)$. 
Here, we adopt the Lao-type profile parametrization \cite{Lao_1985} for the plasma current density.
\begin{align*}
  J_{\phi, \rm{pl}}(R, Z; \psi) =
  \lambda &\left[\beta_0 \frac{R}{R_{\rm{geo}}}\tilde{j}_p(\psi, \psi_a, \psi_b) + \right. \\
  & \ \left. (1-\beta_0) \frac{R_{\rm{geo}}}{R}\tilde{j}_F(\psi, \psi_a, \psi_b)\right]
  \bf{1}_{\Omega_{\rm{p}}}(R, Z),
\end{align*}
where $R_{\te{geo}}$ is the major radius of the plasma;
$\lambda$ and $\beta_0$ are the normalization constants, with details of the 
normalization given in Appendix~\ref{sec:normalization}.
Here $\tilde{j}_p$ and $\tilde{j}_F$ are the normalized pressure and 
toroidal magnetic field profiles: 
\begin{align*}
  \tilde{j}_p(\psi, \psi_a, \psi_b) &= (1 - \psi_s^{\alpha_m})^{\alpha_n} \\
  \tilde{j}_F(\psi, \psi_a, \psi_b) &= (1 - \psi_s^{\beta_m})^{\beta_n} \\
  \psi_s &= \frac{\psi - \psi_a}{\psi_b - \psi_a}, \\
\end{align*}
The parameters $\alpha_m, \alpha_n, \beta_m, \beta_n$ determine  
the shapes of the pressure and toroidal field profiles,
and the quantities $\psi_a$ and $\psi_b$ are the poloidal-flux values
at the plasma magnetic axis and at the plasma boundary, respectively.
We discuss these quantities further in the next subsection.

For later use, we decompose $\psi = \psi_\text{p} + \psi_\text{m}$,
taking advantage of the linearity of  the left-hand side of the Grad-Shafranov equation,
where $\psi_\te{p}$ satisfies
\[ \Delta^* \psi_\te{p}(R, Z) = -\mu_0 R J_{\text{p}, \phi}(R, Z; \psi), \]
and $\psi_\te{m}$ satisfies
\[ \Delta^* \psi_\te{m}(R, Z) = -\mu_0 R J_{\text{m}}(R, Z). \]
Note that $J_{\text{p}, \phi}$ is a function of $\psi$, not $\psi_\te{p}$.

Given $J_\phi$, we can obtain the value of $\psi$ at the computational boundary $\partial \Omega$.
Let $\psi_{\te{bdry}}: \partial \Omega \to \bb{R}$ denote the boundary value of $\psi$ on $\partial \Omega$.

\begin{align}\label{eq:4}
  \psi_{\te{bdry}}(R, Z) = \iint_\Omega G(R, Z; R', Z') J_\phi(R', Z'; \psi) dR' dZ',
\end{align} 
where $(R, Z) \in \partial \Omega$ and $G$ is the Green's function for the operator $\Delta^*$;
\begin{align*} G(R, Z; R', Z') &= \frac{\mu_0}{2 \pi} \frac{\sqrt{R R'}}{k} \left[
  (2 - k^2) K(k) - 2E(k) 
\right], \\ k^2 &= \frac{4 R R'}{(R + R')^2 + (Z - Z')^2} \end{align*}
and $K$ and $E$ are the complete elliptic integrals of the first and second kind, respectively.

\subsubsection{Determination of the plasma boundary} \label{sec:2.1.2}

In the free-boundary problem, the plasma boundary is not known a priori, and
hence it is determined by the solution $\psi$ itself.
More precisely, given the plasma flux $\psi$ and the geometry of the tokamak,
the plasma boundary value $\psi_b$ is determined by the following procedure.
Assume the total plasma current 
\[ I_p = \int_{\Omega_p} J_{\text{p}, \phi}(R, Z; \psi) \, dR \, dZ > 0. \]
Then the poloidal flux $\psi$ typically attains a minimum at the plasma magnetic axis, and increases toward the plasma boundary.
If a saddle point of $\psi$, called the X-point, exists inside the tokamak structure, 
the plasma boundary value is determined at the value of $\psi$ at the X-point.  
In this case, we say that the plasma is diverted. 
The minimum value of $\psi$ is attained at the plasma magnetic axis, denoted by $\psi_a$
as mentioned above.
Therefore, we identify candidate critical points where $|\nabla \psi|$ is close to zero,
and we then compute the Hessian matrix of $\psi$ at each candidate point to classify it as 
either the magnetic axis or the X-point.
Then the value of $\psi$ at the X-point is the plasma boundary value $\psi_b$, and 
the value of $\psi$ at the magnetic axis is $\psi_a$. 
For $I_p < 0$, the roles of local minimum and maximum are reversed; 
in particular, the plasma magnetic axis corresponds to the local maximum of $\psi$. 

On the other hand, because the domain $\Omega$ is bounded, in some cases 
all saddle points of $\psi$ may lie outside the tokamak structure, and hence the plasma boundary is
determined by the limiter, which is a material structure of the tokamak.
In this case, the plasma boundary value $\psi_b$ is determined by the largest/smallest value of $\psi$ on the limiter-facing surface when $I_p > 0$/$I_p < 0$. In this case, the plasma is limited.

Finally, the plasma boundary is the level set of $\psi_b$;
\[ \partial \Omega_p = \{(R, Z) \in \Omega: \psi(R, Z) = \psi_b\}. \]
See Figure~\ref{fig:boundary} for the visualization of the plasma boundary.

Given the plasma boundary, the plasma current density $J_{\text{p}, \phi}$ 
and the correspoinding boundary value of $\psi_{\te{bdry}}$ 
are obtained by \eqref{eq:3} and \eqref{eq:4}, respectively.


\subsection{Numerical methods for solving the Grad-Shafranov equation}

Because $J_\phi$ depends on $\psi$, the equation is nonlinear. 
Thus, the numerical solution of the Grad-Shafranov equation is obtained
by iteratively solving the equation with an updated $J_\phi$ until convergence.

In particular, we start with an initial guess $\psi^{(0)}$.
Given this initial guess, suppose that the previous iterate $\psi^{(n-1)}$
is available for $n \geq 1$.
Then we first determine the plasma domain $\Omega_p^{(n-1)}$ from $\psi^{(n-1)}$, 
and we compute the toroidal current density $J_\phi(\cdot ; \psi^{(n-1)})$ 
on the computational domain $\Omega$ and $\psi_{\te{bdry}}^{(n-1)}$ at $\partial \Omega$ 
with the plasma domain $\Omega_p^{(n-1)}$ and the previous iterate $\psi^{(n-1)}$.
Because the plasma current density is recomputed from the updated
current density, note that the newly obtained 
$\psi_{\te{bdry}}^{(n-1)}$ need not coincide with the $\psi^{(n-1)}$ on the boundary $\partial \Omega$. 
Then we can obtain the solution $\psi^{(n)}$ by solving the one-step 
Grad-Shafranov equation;
\begin{align}\label{eq:5}
  \left\{
  \begin{aligned}
    \Delta^* \psi^{(n)}(R, Z) &= -\mu_0 R J_\phi(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)}), \quad (R, Z) \in \Omega, \\
    \psi^{(n)}(R, Z) &= \psi_{\text{bdry}}^{(n-1)}(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)}), \quad (R, Z) \in \partial \Omega,
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
\end{align}
and we repeat the above procedure until convergence. 
For later use, we formulate it in the operator form. 
Let $\cal{F}$ be the one-step solution operator, defined by
\[ \cal{F}[\psi^{(n-1)}] = \psi^{(n)}, \]
where $\psi^{(n)}$ is the solution of the one-step Grad-Shafranov equation \eqref{eq:5} with 
$J_\phi(\cdot ; \psi^{(n-1)})$ and $\psi_{\text{bdry}}^{(n-1)}$.
Then its fixed point is the exact solution for the Grad-Shafranov equation.
% Equivalently, we consider the residual operator $\cal{R}$ defined as 
% $\cal{R}[\psi] = \psi - \cal{F}[\psi]$, and the solution
% of the Grad-Shafranov equation is the root of $\cal{R}.$

Several numerical methods can be used to solve the one-step Grad-Shafranov equation.
Since in most cases the computational domain $\Omega$ is the extended area of the 
tokamak structure, its geometry is often simple.
Also the differential operator $\Delta^*$ is linear.
Thus standard finite difference methods with central differences are widely used.
However, in general $\cal{F}$ is not a contraction, and several studies 
\cite{multisolns2,multsolns} have reported that there are multiple solutions 
for the Grad-Shafranov equation, and hence the convergence of the above procedure is not guaranteed.
% For example, with 

Alternatively, when $\psi^{(n)}$ is close to the solution, 
the Jacobian-free Newton-Krylov method can be used to solve the one-step Grad-Shafranov equation.
Because the boundary computation for the plasma is included to compute $\cal{F}$,
it is difficult to compute its Jacobian matrix with respect to $\psi$.
Meanwhile, the standard Newton Krylov method only requires the matrix-vector product of the Jacobian matrix, not the Jacobian matrix itself.
Therefore, we approximate the Jacobian matrix $\cal{J}[\psi](\Delta \psi)$ 
by the finite difference method;
\[ \cal{J}[\psi](\Delta \psi) \approx 
\frac{\cal{F}[\psi + \epsilon \Delta \psi] - \cal{F}[\psi]}{\epsilon} \]
for $\epsilon > 0$ a small constant.
We perform Arnoldi iteration with this formulation to compute the Krylov subspace,
and solve the least squares problem to obtain the update direction $\Delta \psi$.
(Details of the Jacobian-free Newton-Krylov method are provided in Appendix~\ref{sec:JFNK}.)

Our method is designed to serve as a neural surrogate 
for the numerical solver, to replace the operator $\cal{F}$ so the overall 
procedure can be accelerated.
% , as we will describe in the next section.
In this work, our neural network assisted approach is combined to the 
FreeGSNKE solver \cite{freegsnke},
which uses both a finite difference method and a Jacobian-free Newton-Krylov 
method to solve the one-step Grad-Shafranov equation.

\subsection{Physics-Informed Neural Operators}

Neural operators \cite{DeepONet,FNO} are a class of neural networks designed to learn mappings between function spaces.
Let $\cal{X}$ and $\cal{Y}$ be Banach spaces of functions defined on the bounded domain. 
Our task is to approximate an operator 
\[ \cal{N}: \cal{X} \to \cal{Y}, \]
using a neural network $\cal{N}_\theta$.
In general, to train the neural operator, one typically uses a dataset 
$\{(v_i, u_i = \cal{N}(v_i)) \}_{i=1}^N$ where $v_i \in \cal{X}$ and $u_i \in \cal{Y}$ 
are the input and output functions, respectively.
Then the neural operator $\cal{N}_\theta$ is trained to minimize the loss function 
\[ L(\theta) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \|\cal{N}_\theta(v_i) - u_i \|^2 \]
where the norm is defined on the function space $\cal{Y}$, and in practice, 
it is approximated by the discrete $L^2$ norm on the grid points.

Physics-informed neural operators (PINOs) \cite{PIDeepONet,PIFNO} combine 
the neural operator framework with a physics-informed loss function, 
which allows them to learn without relying on solution data. 
Consider a general parametrized elliptic PDE with the form 
\begin{align}\label{eq:6}
  \begin{cases}
    \cal{L}[u](x) = f(x;v), & x \in \Omega, \\
    \cal{B}[u](x) = g(x;v), & x \in \partial \Omega.
  \end{cases}
\end{align}
Here, $v \in \cal{X}$ affects the source term $f$ and the boundary condition $g$, and 
$u \in \cal{Y}$ is the solution of the PDE, $\cal{L}$ is the differential operator, and
$\cal{B}$ is the boundary operator.
The goal is to learn the operator $\cal{N}: \cal{X} \to \cal{Y}$ which maps
$v$ to $u$, with the input data $\{ v_i \}_{i=1}^{N_\te{data}}$ and the governing PDE, 
without access to the corresponding solutions $\{u_i\}_{i=1}^N$.

The physics-informed loss function can be written as
\begin{align}\label{eq:7}
  \begin{aligned}
    L(\theta) &= L_\te{r}(\theta) + L_\te{b}(\theta) \\
    L_\te{r}(\theta) &= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \int_\Omega |\cal{L}[\cal{N}_\theta(v_i)](x) - f(x;v_i)|^2 dx, \\
    L_\te{b}(\theta) &= \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \int_{\partial \Omega} |\cal{B}[\cal{N}_\theta(v_i)](x) - g(x;v_i)|^2 dx.
  \end{aligned}
\end{align}
In our case, for a given previous iterate $\psi^{(n-1)}\in \cal{X} \subset H^2(\Omega)$, 
the one-step Grad-Shafranov solution belongs to 
$\cal{Y}_{\psi^{(n-1)}} = \{ u \in H^2(\Omega) : 
u|_{\partial \Omega} = \psi_{\te{bdry}}|_{\partial \Omega} \}$,
where $H^2(\te{int }\Omega)$ denotes the Sobolev space of functions with 
square-integrable second derivatives. 
Also for $\psi^{(n-1)} \in \cal{X}$,
\begin{align*}
  \cal{L} &= \Delta^*, \quad \cal{B}[\psi^{(n-1)}] = \psi^{(n-1)}_{\te{bdry}},  \\
  f(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)}) &= -\mu_0 R J_\phi (R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)}),  \\
  g(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)}) &= \psi_{\te{bdry}}(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)}).
\end{align*} 

In this work, we use a combination of physics-informed DeepONet 
and the physics-informed neural transformer operator,
to predict the solution of the one-step Grad-Shafranov equation.
We train these two models using the physics-informed formulation described above. 
In the next two subsections, the architectures of the two models are described in detail.

\subsubsection{Physics-Informed DeepONet}

DeepONet \cite{DeepONet} is one of the most widely used neural operators, 
and it is based on the universal approximation theorem for operators. 
Its structure is composed of two sub-networks, called the branch net and the trunk net.
The branch net $b = b_{\theta_b}: \bb{R}^m \to \bb{R}^p$ takes discrete samples of the input function $v$, i.e., $v_D = \left[
  v(x_1), \cdots, v(x_m)
\right]^\top \in \bb{R}^m$, where $x_i$ are the sensor locations in the domain of $v$,
and the trunk net $t = t_{\theta_t}: \bb{R}^d \to \bb{R}^p$ takes the coordinates $x \in \bb{R}^d$ as the input. 
Then the output of the DeepONet is given by
\[ \cal{N}_\theta[v](x) = \sum_{i=1}^p b_i(v_D) t_i (x), \]
where $\theta = (\theta_b, \theta_t)$ are the trainable parameters,
and $b_i$ and $t_i$ are the $i$-th component of the output of branch net and trunk net, respectively.

Because the trunk net depends on the coordinates, we track the derivatives of the output 
with respect to the coordinates $x$ by automatic differentiation \cite{autodiff}. 
Hence, we are able to learn the DeepONet without access to the solution of the PDE,
as long as the input function $\psi$ is given. 
This is the main idea of the physics-informed DeepONet (PI-DeepONet) \cite{PIDeepONet}. 
We utilize the PI-DeepONet as the one-step Grad-Shafranov solver for $\psi$.

\subsubsection{Physics-Informed Neural Transformer Operator}

Transformer \cite{transformer} has become a standard backbone for state-of-the-art models in 
many research areas, such as natural language processing (NLP) and computer vision.
Its core structure is the multi-head self-attention mechanism.
With input sequence $X=[x_1,\ldots,x_n]^\top \in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$, 
positional encoding is added to the input to obtain $H^{(0)}$.
Next, each layer computes the queries $Q_h=H^{(\ell-1)} W_h^Q$, keys $K_h=H^{(\ell-1)} W_h^K$,
and values $V_h=H^{(\ell-1)} W_h^V$, for each attention head $h$.
Here $W_h^Q, W_h^K$, and $W_h^V$ are learnable parameters, and $H^{(\ell-1)}$ is 
the output of the previous layer.
Then the scaled dot-product attention follows:
\[ \te{Attn}_h\left(H^{(\ell-1)}\right)=\te{softmax}\left(Q_hK_h^\top/\sqrt{d_k}\right)V_h, \]
where $\te{softmax}(x) = \frac{e^x}{\sum_{i=1}^n e^{x_i}}$ and $d_k$ is the dimension of the keys.
The outputs of multiple heads are concatenated and projected as 
\[ \mathrm{MHA}(H^{(\ell-1)})=\mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{Attn}_1,\ldots,\mathrm{Attn}_m)W^O. \]
Here $W^O$ is a learnable parameter, and $m$ is the number of attention heads.
Then the layer normalization and feed-forward network are applied to the output of the multi-head attention, 
to obtain $H^{(\ell)}$ for the next layer.

With this structure in mind, we introduce a physics-informed neural transformer 
operator (PINTO) \cite{PINTO}, which is a successful transformer-based neural operator architecture.
Unlike a standard transformer, PINTO takes three inputs:
\begin{itemize}
  \item first, the query input is the coordinate $x\in \bb{R}^{d\times 1}$ 
  where we want to predict the solution,
  \item second, the key input is the vector consisting of the discretization points 
  $X_D = [x_{b, 1}, \cdots, x_{b, N}] \in \bb{R}^{d \times N}$ 
  on the boundary $\partial \Omega$, and
  \item third, the value input is the vector consisting of the boundary values 
  $g_D = [g(x_{b, 1}; v), \cdots, g(x_{b, N}; v)] \in \bb{R}^{1 \times N}$ 
  at those discretization points.
\end{itemize} 

All inputs are encoded with a fully-connected layer so that they have the same dimension $p$,
i.e., 
\begin{align*}
  H_q^{(0)} &= \te{MLP}_q(x) \in \bb{R}^{1 \times p}, \\
  H_k^{(0)} &= \te{MLP}_k(X_D) \in \bb{R}^{N\times p}, \\
  H_v^{(0)} &= \te{MLP}_v(g_D) \in \bb{R}^{N\times p},
\end{align*}
and then the attention is computed with the above formula;
\begin{align*}
  &Q_h = (H_q^{(0)}) W_h^Q \in \bb{R}^{1 \times p}, \\ 
  &K_h = (H_{k, i}^{(0)}) W_h^K \in \bb{R}^{N \times p}, \\
  &V_h = (H_{v, i}^{(0)}) W_h^V \in \bb{R}^{N \times p}, \\
  &\te{Attn}_h \left(H^{(0)}\right) = \te{softmax}\left(Q_h K_h^\top / \sqrt{p}\right) V_h \in \bb{R}^{1 \times N}, \\
  &\mathrm{MHA}(H^{(0)}) = \mathrm{Concat}(\mathrm{Attn}_1, \ldots, \mathrm{Attn}_m) W^O \in \bb{R}^{1\times p},
\end{align*}
where $W_h^Q \in \bb{R}^{p \times d_k}$, $W_h^K \in \bb{R}^{p \times d_k}$, $W_h^V \in \bb{R}^{p \times d_v}$, and $W^O \in \bb{R}^{mp \times p}$ are learnable parameters, and $\te{Concat}$ is the concatenation operation.

To obtain the next layer input $H^{(1)}$, the small MLP layer with a skip connection is used:
\[ H^{(1)} = \te{MLP}\left(H_q^{(0)} + MHA(H^{(0)})\right) \in \bb{R}^{1 \times p},\]
and the similar procedure with $H_q^{(1)} = H_k^{(1)} = H_v^{(1)} = H^{(1)}$ is
repeated in the subsequent layers.
Finally, a decoder MLP is applied to obtain the scalar output,
\[ \cal{N}_\theta[v](x) = \te{MLP}_\te{dec}((H^{(L)})^\top) \in \bb{R}^{1}, \]
which approximates the solution at the query point $x$.

\section{Method}

In this section, we present the training procedures for the neural operators and explain how they
are integrated within the overall algorithm for solving the free-boundary Grad-Shafranov equation. 
Hereafter, we refer to the proposed method as FreeGSNO.

\subsection{Training procedure of neural operators}
Let us consider the one-step Grad-Shafranov equation \eqref{eq:5}.
Because the operator $\Delta^*$ is linear, we can decompose the solution into the
residual part and the boundary part;
\begin{align}\label{eq:9}&\left\{
  \begin{aligned}
    \Delta^*\psi_r^{(n)}(R, Z) &= -\mu_0 R J_\phi(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)}),& \quad &(R, Z) \in \Omega, \\
    \psi_r^{(n)}(R, Z) &= 0, & &(R, Z) \in \partial \Omega,
  \end{aligned}\right. \\
  \label{eq:10}&\left\{
  \begin{aligned}
    \Delta^*\psi_b^{(n)}(R, Z) &= 0, &\quad &(R, Z) \in \Omega, \\
    \psi_b^{(n)}(R, Z) &= \psi_{\te{bdry}}(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)}), & & (R, Z) \in \partial \Omega.
  \end{aligned}
  \right.
\end{align}
Then it is clear that $\psi^{(n)} = \psi_r^{(n)} + \psi_b^{(n)}$.

First, for \eqref{eq:9}, we use the modified PI-DeepONet to learn the operator 
\[ \cal{N}_r: \cal{X} \to \cal{Y}_0, \  \cal{N}_r [\psi^{(n-1)}] = \psi_r^{(n)}, \]
where $\cal{Y}_0 = \{ u \in H^2(\Omega) : u|_{\partial \Omega} = 0 \}$.
We need to encode the source term $-\mu_0 R J_\phi(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)})$.
To do this, we discretize the source term $-\mu_0 R J_\phi(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)})$ on the grid
points of the domain $\Omega$, 
and multiply a normalization constant $J_{\te{norm}}$
to obtain the branch net input $J_D \in [0, 1]^{N_R \times N_Z}$.
Here $N_R$ and $N_Z$ are the number of grid points in the $R$ and $Z$ direction, respectively.
Since the discretized source term has the structure of two-dimensional image data,
we use a convolutional neural network (CNN) with one channel input 
as the branch net of the PI-DeepONet to encode the source term, to obtain 
\[ b = b(J_D) = \te{CNN}(J_D) \in \bb{R}^p. \]

On the other hand, the trunk net is a simple MLP, which encodes the coordinates $(R, Z)$;
\[ t = t(R, Z) =\te{MLP}_t(R, Z) \in \bb{R}^p. \]
In the standard PI-DeepONet formulation, the outputs of the branch net and the trunk net are
combined by the dot product, but in our experiments this dot-product form was not
sufficiently expressive for the Grad-Shafranov solution.
Motivated by NOMAD \cite{NOMAD}, our model concatenates the branch and trunk outputs 
and feeds them into a decoder MLP.
Then we renormalize the output to obtain the final result; 
\[ \cal{N}_r[\psi^{(n-1)}](R, Z) = \frac{1}{J_{\te{norm}}}\te{MLP}_d(b(J_D), t(R, Z)) \in \bb{R}. \]

To train this model, we impose the boundary condition using the hard-constraint formulation.
In $\eqref{eq:7}$, the boundary condition is enforced through a penalty term in the loss function;
this is known as a soft constraint. 
By decomposing the problem, on the other hand, we can impose the zero-boundary condition
for $\psi_r$ directly as a hard-constraint. 
We follow the hard-constraint approach in \cite{hardbdry}, so the solution 
$\psi_r$ is obtained by
\begin{align*}
  \psi_r^{(n)}(R, Z) = \cal{N}_r[\psi^{(n-1)}](R, Z) - H_r[\cal{N}_r[\psi^{(n-1)}]](R, Z),
\end{align*}
where $H_r[v]: \Omega \to \bb{R}$ is a lifting function whose values match
those of $\cal{N}_r[v]$ on $\partial \Omega$,
and hence $\psi_r^{(n)}$ automatically satisfies the zero-boundary condition. 
Then we only need to minimize the residual loss
\[ L(\theta) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \int_\Omega |\Delta^* \psi_r^{(n)}(R, Z)+ \mu_0 R J_\phi(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)})|^2 dR dZ. \]
The hard-constraint method enforces the boundary condition accurately, 
while maintaining a residual loss scale comparable to that of the soft constraint method,
as shown in Section \ref{sec:5}.

Next, for \eqref{eq:10}, we use the PINTO to learn the operator
\[ \cal{N}_b: \cal{X} \to \cal{Y}_{\psi^{(n-1)}}, \ \cal{N}_b [\psi^{(n-1)}] = \psi_b^{(n)}. \]
To do this, we need to encode the boundary value $\psi_{\te{bdry}}$.
Let $X_{\te{bdry}, D} = [(R_{b, i}, Z_{b, i})]_{i=1}^{N_b} \in \bb{R}^{N_b \times 2}$  be the coordinates of the discretization points 
on the boundary $\partial \Omega$, and let 
\begin{align*}
  \psi_{\te{bdry}, D} &= \psi_{\te{bdry}, norm}\left[
  \psi_{\te{bdry}}(R_{b, 1}, Z_{b, 1}; \psi^{(n-1)}), \cdots, \right. \\
  & \left. \psi_{\te{bdry}}(R_{b, N_b}, Z_{b, N_b}; \psi^{(n-1)}) \right]
  \in [0, 1]^{N_b}.
\end{align*}
where $\psi_{\te{bdry}, \te{norm}}$ is the normalization constant for the boundary value.
To evaluate $\psi_b^{(n)}(R, Z)$, we provide $(R, Z)$, $X_{\te{bdry}, D}$ and $\psi_{\te{bdry}, D}$
as the query, keys and values, respectively. After rescaling the normalized output, we have
\begin{align*}
  \hat{\psi}_b^{(n)} &= \cal{N}_b[\psi^{(n-1)}] (R, Z) \\
  &= \frac{1}{\psi_{\te{bdry}, \te{norm}}}\te{PINTO}(R, Z; X_{\te{bdry}, D}, \psi_{\te{bdry}, D}) \in \bb{R}.
\end{align*}
To train PINTO, we use the loss function defined in \eqref{eq:7}. 
Finally, the approximate solution is $\hat{\psi}^{(n)} = \hat{\psi}_r^{(n)} + \hat{\psi}_b^{(n)}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:integrated} shows the structure of the integrated version of the PI-DeepONet
and the PINTO for solving the one-step Grad-Shafranov equation.

We now explain the motivation of dividing the problem into two equations.
ProPINN \cite{propagation} describe the ``propagation failure mode'' 
of the physics-informed neural networks.
For example, in the case of a finite element method, the interior solution is affected
by the boundary values, because the neighboring elements are dependent on each other.
However, in the case of a neural network, the gradient of the model parameter at 
different locations are easily orthogonal,
resulting in an independent optimization of the model parameter at each location and
the failure of the propagation of the boundary information into the interior of the domain.
(Figure 1 of \cite{R3} illustrates this phenomenon.)
% How can I show the 

The transformer architecture is known to be effective for learning  
long-range dependencies, and hence many studies have been conducted for solving the PDEs 
with transformer architecture \cite{PINTO,Transolver,BENO}.
% However, because the cost of the forward pass of the transformer is high,
% in many cases the training is done by data-driven approach.
% On the other hand, 
As discussed above, PINTO \cite{PINTO} combines the transformer architecture,
with physics-informed loss functions.
However, it is primarily designed to handle only different boundary conditions, 
and it is trained and evaluated for a homogeneous source term.

Meanwhile, the Physics-informed DeepONet \cite{DeepONet} architecture is known to be effective
for learning mappings from source terms to solutions, at a lower forward-pass cost 
than transformer-based architectures.
Also, due to the decomposition of the solution, it is easier to impose the 
boundary condition using a hard-constraint method.
Therefore, combining PI-DeepONet with PINTO is a natural framework
for solving the one-step Grad-Shafranov equation.

\subsection{Integration of the Proposed Model with the FreeGSNKE Solver}
The FreeGSNKE solver is a numerical solver for the Grad-Shafranov equation 
implemented as a Python package.
It handles three different problems, the static forward problem, the static inverse problem,
and the evolutive forward problem. 
In this work we only focus on the static forward Grad-Shafranov solver, and 
the extension to other problems is left for a future study. 
More details are provided in Appendix~\ref{sec:freegsnke}.

In the early iterations, FreeGSNKE solver uses the simple fixed-point iteration method, and when
the error is small enough, it switches to the Jacobian-free Newton-Krylov method.
The detailed algorithm is described in Algorithm~\ref{alg:1}.

\begin{algorithm}
  \begin{algorithmic}
    \STATE \textbf{Input:} The initial guess $\psi^{(0)}$, the tolerance $\ep > 0$,
    $n \gets 1$
    \STATE \textbf{Phase 1: Fixed-Point Iteration}
    \WHILE {the stopping criterion is not satisfied}
      \STATE Update the plasma domain $\Omega_p^{(n-1)}$ from $\psi^{(n-1)}$
      \STATE Compute $J_\phi^{(n-1)} = J_\phi^{(n-1)}(R, Z; \psi^{(n-1)})$ 
      \STATE Compute the boundary value $\psi_{\rm{bdry}}^{(n-1)}$ 
      \STATE ``Solve equation'' \eqref{eq:5} to get $\psi^{(n)}$
      \STATE $n \gets n + 1$
    \ENDWHILE
    \STATE \textbf{Phase 2: Newton-Krylov Iteration}
    \WHILE {the stopping criterion is not satisfied}
      \STATE Krylov dimension $d \gets 0$ 
      \WHILE {the Krylov stopping criterion is not satisfied}
        \STATE $d \gets d + 1$
        \STATE Compute the basis $\{q_i\}_{i=1}^d$ by Arnoldi iteration $\gets$ 
        Here we need to compute $\cal{F}$
        \STATE Solve the damped least squares problem to get coefficients $\{c_i\}_{i=1}^d$
      \ENDWHILE
      \STATE Update $\psi^{(n)} = \psi^{(n-1)} + \sum_{i} c_i q_i$
      \STATE $n \gets n + 1$
    \ENDWHILE
    \STATE \textbf{Output:} The solution $\psi = \psi^{(n)}$ 
  \end{algorithmic}
  \caption{Two-Phase Iterative Method for Solving the Grad-Shafranov Equation}
  \label{alg:1}
\end{algorithm}
Note that the steps in the fixed-point iteration is the same step for computing $\cal{F}$.

Our method replaces the step ``solve equation'' in the above algorithm
and the corresponding part in the Arnoldi iteration  
for the Jacobian-free Newton-Krylov method, with our neural operator model.
In the next section, we will show the numerical results of our method, and compare it with the original Freegsnke solver.

\section{Experiments} \label{sec:5}

We conduct several numerical experiments to validate the performance of FreeGSNO.

\subsection{Solov'ev equilibrium}

Solov'ev equilibrium assumes that the toroidal current density does not depend on
the poloidal flux $\psi$, and therefore it admits an analytical solution,
and this problem is widely used to validate numerical solvers.
In this case, the Grad-Shafranov equation has a simple form of the toroidal 
current density, 
\[ J_\phi(R, Z) = \frac{1}{\mu_0 R} (- A_1 R^2 + A_2), \]
and the equation is reduced to a linear PDE.
Then the solution $\psi^*$ can be expressed as a linear combination of the basis functions \cite{Solovev}
$\{\psi_i\}_{i=1}^\infty$ and the particular solution of the PDE, as follows:
\[ \psi^*(R, Z) = \sum_{i=1}^\infty c_i \psi_i(R, Z) + \frac{A_1}{8}R^4 - \frac{A_2}{2}Z^2.\]
where the coefficients $\{c_i\}_{i=1}^\infty$ are determined by the boundary condition.

We follow \cite{Solovev} and we use the first 4 basis functions 
\[ \psi_1 = 1, \psi_2 = R^2, \psi_3 = Z^2 - R^2 \log R, \psi_4 = R^4 - 4R^2 Z^2. \]
The coefficients $\{c_i\}_{i=1}^4$ are determined from the values of the $\psi$ at 
selected major boundary points;
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\psi(R_i, 0) = \psi(R_o, 0) = 0$,
  \item $\psi(R_t, Z_t) = 0$, 
  \item $\partial_R \psi(R_t, Z_t) = 0$,
\end{itemize}
where $R_i$ is the inner radius, $R_o$ is the outer radius, and $(R_t, Z_t)$ is the 
location of the topmost point of the plasma. 
Figure \ref{fig} shows some examples of the solution of the Solov'ev equilibrium with the above parameters and its corresponding plasma boundary.

We set the computational domain $\Omega$ as $[0.5, 1.5] \times [-0.6, 0.6]$,
without the tokamak geometry.
Then we sample 7,500 parameter tuples $A_1, A_2 \in [0, 1], R_o \in [1.2, 1.3], R_i \in [0.7, 0.8], R_t \in [0.7, 1.0]$, and $Z_t \in [0.2, 0.54]$,
with each parameter chosen uniformly at random, and we prune some samples 
so that $R_i < R_t < R_o$, $J_\phi$ does not change sign in the plasma region, and 
we discard samples with excessively large source terms to stabilize training.
We discretize the domain with $N_R = 65$ and $N_Z = 65$ grid points 
to compute the toroidal current density and the boundary value for the training of the neural operators.

Then we train the neural operators with the following architecture details.
First, the branch net for the modified PI-DeepONet has three convolutional layers with 32, 64, and 64 channels.
Each convolutional layer has kernel size 3 and stride 1, and global max pooling and a 
ReLU activation function are applied after each convolutional layer.
Then the output of the last convolutional layer is flattened and fed into two  
fully-connected layers, with 1024 and 256 hidden units with ReLU activation function.
We note that the branch net architecture is not related to the structure of the PDE,
and we use a larger branch net than the trunk net,
Second, the trunk net of the modified PI-DeepONet utilizes the Fourier feature mapping \cite{FFM},
followed by 2 fully-connected layers with 64 hidden units.
The activation function is a sine function \cite{activationsine} with phase shift;
\[ \sigma(x) = a \sin(x + b), \]
where $a$ and $b$ are trainable parameters.
Third, the dimension of the latent space (i.e., the output dimension of the branch and trunk net) is 30. The decoder for the modified PI-DeepONet has a fully-connected layer with
64 hidden units, and the activation function is the same as the trunk net.

Meanwhile, each input to PINTO is encoded by a fully-connected layer with 32 hidden units 
and SiLU activation function, to make a hidden representation of dimension 30.
Then the multi-head attention with two heads is performed, and after each multi-head attention 
block, the multi-layer perceptron with 64 hidden units and SiLU activation function is applied. 
The skip connection is applied to each layer, and there are 2 multi-head attention layers in total.
The output is decoded by a fully-connected layer with 32 hidden units and SiLU activation function.

At training, we use adam optimizers for both models, with the initial learning rate of $10^{-3}$,
and the exponential decay scheduler with the decay rate of 0.9. 
We train the models for 5000 epochs, and as we described above, the loss function for the 
residual net (PI-DeepONet) is only the residual loss, while the loss function for the boundary net
(PINTO) is the weighted sum of the residual loss and the boundary loss, 
with the weight of 10 for the boundary loss, i.e.,
\[ L(\theta) = \frac{1}{1 + w_\te{bdry}} L_\te{PDE} +
\frac{w_\te{bdry}}{1 + w_\te{bdry}} L_\te{bdry}. \] 
with $w_\te{bdry} = 10$.
The final training loss is $5.868 \times 10^{-3}$ for the residual net, and $1.130 \times 10^{-3}$
for the boundary net.

For testing, we sample 1,000 parameter tuples with the same distribution as the training set, 
and we compute the absolute and relative $L^2$ error between the analytic solution and the predicted solution by the neural operators.
Figure \ref{fig:1} shows the comparison of the analytic solution and 
the predicted solution by the neural operators.
We observe that the neural operators can predict the solution of the Grad-Shafranov equation with 
high accuracy, and it can capture the plasma boundary well.

\subsection{MAST-U tokamak equilibrium}\label{sec:5.2}

To further validate the performance of our method in the free-boundary Grad-Shafranov problem, 
we conduct an experiment for the MAST-U tokamak equilibrium.
Mega Ampere Spherical Tokamak Upgrade (MAST-U) \cite{MASTU} is a spherical tokamak located in the United Kingdom.
It is designed to develop the physics knowledge for the spherical tokamak design, to extend
physics understanding of the fusion programs.
For the equilibrium reconstruction modeling of MAST-U, we need the following three major 
machine components as described in the below;
\begin{itemize}
  \item active poloidal field coils,
  \item passive structures, and
  \item limiter/wall structure.
\end{itemize}
MAST-U contains 12 active poloidal field coils, 150 passive structures 
(i.e., the vessel, gas baffles, coil cases, etc.), and a limiter structure.
See the right figure of Figure~\ref{fig:tokamak} for the locations of the structures and 
see \cite{validation} for the detailed description of the MAST-U machine.

FreeGSNKE solver has options to model each current of the active poloidal field coils,
refine and distribute the currents of the passive structures, and model the limiter structure
as a set of coordinate pairs that form the closed polygon.
We note that the FreeGSNKE is not restricted to the MAST-U machine and 
be able to model any tokamak machine with different machine descriptions;

To train the neural operators for the MAST-U equilibrium, 
we set the computational domain $\Omega$ as 
$[0.1 \te{m}, 2.0 \te{m}]  \times [-2.2 \te{m}, 2.2 \te{m}]$ 
and we discretize the domain with $N_R = 65$ and $N_Z = 129$ grid points.
Then, we sample 100 parameter tuples
$(p_{\te{axis}}, I_p)$ where $p_{\te{axis}}$ is the pressure at the magnetic axis and 
$I_p$ is the total plasma current. 
The parameter 
\[ \beta_\te{pol} = \frac{8\pi}{\mu_0} \frac{\langle p \rangle}{I_p^2} \sim 
\frac{\langle p \rangle}{I_p^2}, \]
where $\langle p \rangle$ is the volume-averaged plasma pressure, is a dimensionless
parameter that represents the ratio of the plasma pressure to the poloidal magnetic field pressure. 
Hence it affects the shape of the plasma region, and it is reasonable to sample 
the parameters near the typical value of $\beta_\te{pol}$ for the MAST-U machine. 
Thus, we follow the example of the FreeGSNKE solver and
we let $\beta_{\te{ratio}} = 0.22 \times 10^{-2} \te{Pa} / \te{kA}^2$ and we sample 
the parameters $(p_{\te{axis}}, I_p)$ from the uniform distribution with 
the range of $I_p \in [550\te{kA}, 650\te{kA}] $ and 
$p_{\te{axis}} \in [0.6 \beta_{\te{ratio}} I_p^2, 1.4 \beta_{\te{ratio}}  I_p^2]$. 
(We fix the currents of the active coils and we set the passive structure currents as zero
to focus on the static forward Grad-Shafranov problem. 
But we note that our method can be extended to the case with different 
currents of the active coils and passive structures.)

Then we run the FreeGSNKE solver, with the discretized domain and 
varying sampled physical parameters, to obtain 
the toroidal current density and the boundary value as the operator 
$\cal{F}$ is computed in both the fixed-point iteration and the Jacobian-free Newton-Krylov method.
These quantities are used for the training of the neural operators.
We set the other configuration of the FreeGSNKE solver as the default setting.
For example, the changing criterion from the fixed-point iteration 
to the Jacobian-free Newton-Krylov method is the value
\[ \frac{\max \left( \Delta \psi^{(n)} \right) - \min \left( \Delta \psi^{(n)} \right)}
{\max\left(\psi^{(n)}\right) - \min\left(\psi^{(n)}\right)} 
\] is below $0.1$, where $\Delta \psi^{(n)} = \psi^{(n)} - \psi^{(n-1)}$,
and the stopping criterion for the overall iteration is the value of the
above quantity is below $10^{-9}$.
We obtain 6,299 samples for the training set, and 700 samples for the test set. 

The training procedure for the neural operators is similar as the Solov'ev equilibrium case,
but the total epochs is 1,000 because the MAST-U dataset is larger than the Solov'ev equilibrium 
case and convergence is observed within fewer epochs.
Figure \ref{fig:loss} shows for the training loss progress of the residual net and the boundary net,
and Table~\ref{tab:1} compares the value of $\psi$ and the location of the X-point,
plasma boundary value, and the value of $\psi$ and the location of the magnetic axis, 
between the FreeGSNO and FreeGSNKE solvers for the MAST-U tokamak equilibrium 
with different physical parameters $(p_{\text{axis}}, I_p)$. 
Also, Figure~\ref{fig:4} shows the comparison of the predicted solution by the neural operators and
the solution by the FreeGSNKE solver.
We observe that the neural operators, combined with the Jacobian-free Newton-Krylov method, 
converge to a solution with high accuracy, and it can capture the plasma boundary well.

\section{Summary, Conclusions and Future Work}
In this work, we propose FreeGSNO, a method that uses neural operators 
to solve the free-boundary Grad-Shafranov equation.
The proposed method is based on the decomposition of the solution into the residual part and the boundary part, and we use the PI-DeepONet for the residual part and the PINTO for the boundary part.
We train the neural operators with only physics-informed loss functions, therefore
our method does not require any labeled data, to expect the physically meaningful solution.
Then we combine the neural operators with the iteration method, 
to solve the Grad-Shafranov equation in each iteration step.

We conduct several numerical experiments to validate the performance of the proposed method.
First, we consider the Solov'ev equilibrium problem, which is a fixed-boundary 
Grad-Shafranov problem that has an analytical solution, 
and we show that the neural operators capture the solution with high accuracy.
Next, we conduct the MAST-U tokamak equilibrium simulation, which is a realistic free-boundary 
Grad-Shafranov problem, and we show that the combination of the neural operators and the
traditional iteration method converges to a solution with high accuracy,
with similar iterations and computation time.
In particular, FreeGSNO captures the plasma boundary and the magnetic axis well,
which is important for the equilibrium reconstruction problem in the tokamak device.

The proposed method shows promising results, therefore we further plan to 
extend the method to the inverse problem and the evolutive problem, and we will
conduct more experiments with different tokamak machines and different physical parameters.
Also, with further implementation and hardware optimization, we can further improve the 
speed of the proposed method so the neural operators are faster than the traditional method,
with training only once.
